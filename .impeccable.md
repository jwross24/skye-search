# Design Context

## Users
**Primary user**: Chinese-born PhD environmental scientist (ocean color remote sensing), navigating STEM OPT unemployment clock, H1-B sponsorship, and EB-2 NIW green card backlog. Under real immigration deadline pressure — PostDoc ending April 2026, 119 unemployment days remaining out of 150.

**Emotional state**: Anxious about visa timelines, overwhelmed by the intersection of job search + immigration complexity, but determined and resourceful. Needs a tool that feels like a calm, knowledgeable ally — not another source of stress.

**Context of use**: Daily check-ins on phone and laptop. Scans job picks over morning coffee. Deep sessions on weekends for applications. Immigration HQ checked when anxiety spikes about deadlines.

## Brand Personality
**Warm companion, not clinical dashboard.** Three words: *Grounding. Encouraging. Clear.*

Like a trusted friend who happens to understand immigration law and academic hiring. The interface should lower heart rate, not raise it. Every screen says "you've got this, here's what matters today."

## Aesthetic Direction
**Warm editorial** — like a curated research briefing from someone who cares about you. Not a job board. Not enterprise SaaS.

- **Color**: Ocean blues (trust, her scientific domain), jade greens (growth, her jade business), warm ambers (gentle urgency). Never red for deadlines — amber signals "pay attention" without triggering stress.
- **Typography**: Plus Jakarta Sans (round-edged, warm, generous line spacing). Geist Mono for code/data contexts only.
- **Space**: Generous whitespace. Left-aligned, asymmetric layouts. No cards-in-cards. Content breathes.
- **Tone**: Short, human sentences. "34 positions ranked by immigration fit" not "Displaying 34 job results sorted by composite urgency metric."

## Design Principles

1. **Immigration context is the product** — Every job card shows why it matters for *her* visa situation. "University — cap-exempt" is more important than the salary.

2. **Urgency without anxiety** — Use warm amber tide-lines and gentle language ("Top priority", "Worth exploring") instead of red alerts and countdown timers. The clock is real; the UI shouldn't amplify the dread.

3. **Progressive disclosure** — Start with what matters today (job picks, days remaining). Reveal complexity through interaction, not upfront data dumps. The "What If" scenarios, attorney exports, and Plan B/C/D details live behind intentional navigation.

4. **Epistemic honesty** — Show confidence levels on visa classifications (confirmed vs. likely vs. unverified). Show data sources on immigration dates (DSO-confirmed vs. user-reported). Never present uncertain data as certain — the stakes are too high.

5. **One user, done right** — Design for Skye first. Every decision optimized for her specific situation (Chinese-born, environmental science PhD, Boston area, cap-exempt priority). Multi-user abstraction comes later, if ever.
